## Executive Summary

Fear is the dominant market emotion — and for once, it's justified. The Iran-US military conflict has created a textbook stagflationary shock: oil +84% YoY, equities selling off, credit spreads widening, and the VIX sitting at the 85th percentile. The CNN Fear & Greed Index at 13 screams capitulation, and AAII bearish sentiment at 51.4% (bull-bear spread at the 4.8th percentile) confirms retail investors have thrown in the towel.

But here's the critical nuance: **the crowd is not wrong — they're early.** The Strait of Hormuz deadline (Apr 7-8) is a binary event that hasn't resolved. The oil shock's inflationary consequences haven't fully appeared in CPI data yet. Credit stress is building but hasn't cascaded. This is fear in the *escalation phase*, not the *resolution phase*. Contrarian buying signals (extreme fear readings) are unreliable when the underlying shock is intensifying, not fading.

**Conviction: Medium-High.** The direction is clear — fear dominates — but the magnitude and duration depend entirely on geopolitical outcomes that are fundamentally unpredictable.

## Behavioral Bias Assessment

**Active Biases (Ranked by Impact):**

1. **Recency Bias (High):** The oil shock is dominating all decision-making. Market participants are extrapolating the past 4 weeks of crude surges (+68% MoM) into permanent regime change. This bias supports the fear trade but also creates vulnerability to a sharp reversal if supply normalizes.

2. **Herding (High):** Fear & Greed at 13 and AAII at 4.8th percentile confirm crowd behavior. The rush into gold (+59% YoY) and out of risk assets is textbook herding. When 90%+ of participants are on one side of the boat, the risk of a violent snap-back exists — though the trigger matters.

3. **Availability Bias (High):** The Iran conflict is visually and emotionally salient — fighter jets, missile strikes, shipping lane threats. This makes the geopolitical risk feel larger than it may objectively be (base case: contained conflict, 35-40% probability per macro analysts).

4. **Anchoring (Medium):** Investors anchored to the soft-landing narrative of late 2025 / early 2026 are struggling to reprice. This creates a slow-motion de-risking rather than a clean capitulation — equities are only –4.7% off, which is mild given an 84% oil shock. The anchoring to "stocks always recover" is delaying full risk reduction.

5. **Loss Aversion (Medium):** Retail investors likely holding underwater positions (BTC –45% from ATH, S&P –6% from highs) are trapped, unwilling to sell at losses, creating a latent supply overhang. If the Hormuz deadline triggers further selling, forced liquidation could accelerate.

6. **Confirmation Bias (Low-Medium):** Bullish investors are finding reasons to stay invested (RSI not oversold, earnings not yet hit, yield curve normalizing). Bearish investors are confirming their thesis with every oil uptick. Both sides are entrenched.

## Forward Sentiment Outlook

### Near-Term (0-4 Weeks)

**Base Case (55%): Elevated fear with binary event risk.**

The Hormuz deadline is the fulcrum. If rhetoric escalates without military action, expect VIX to spike toward 28-30, equities to test 6,300 support, and gold to reclaim $5,000+. If de-escalation signals emerge (back-channel talks, OPEC+ surge), expect a sharp relief rally — S&P could bounce 5-8% in days as shorts cover and fear subsides.

The key behavioral dynamic: **positioning is defensive but not fully capitulated.** There are still trapped longs in equities and crypto. A negative Hormuz trigger could flush these out, creating a washout bottom. A positive trigger creates a short-squeeze rally.

**Sentiment trajectory:** Fear → Fear (if escalation) or Fear → Cautious Optimism (if de-escalation). No path to neutrality in 4 weeks.

### Medium-Term (1-3 Months)

**Base Case (45%): Persistent fear with stagflation overhang.**

Even under a diplomatic resolution scenario, the inflation damage is done. April CPI will show the oil shock feeding through. The Fed will be forced to acknowledge that 2.4% PCE is unachievable in 2026. This keeps policy restrictive and sentiment cautious.

Under a contained conflict scenario (oil $100-120), markets settle into a grind: equities flat to slightly negative, credit spreads slowly widening, gold holding bid. Sentiment shifts from *acute fear* to *chronic pessimism* — less volatile but more corrosive.

**Behavioral risk:** Recency bias fades, replaced by normalization bias — participants accept higher oil and inflation as "the new normal." This is actually more dangerous because vigilance drops while the stagflationary drag compounds.

**Sentiment trajectory:** Fear → Pessimism/Caution. A slow, grinding mood that erodes risk appetite without triggering panic.

### Long-Term (3-12 Months)

**Base Case (40%): Resolution-driven sentiment recovery.**

Most geopolitical conflicts of this nature are resolved within 6-12 months through diplomatic channels, military exhaustion, or regime change. When the Iran situation eventually resolves, the sentiment recovery will be sharp — oil normalizes, inflation expectations re-anchor, and the Fed resumes easing.

The question is how much economic damage accumulates before resolution. If growth slows to 1.5% with inflation at 3%, the "stagflation scar" lingers even after the conflict ends. Sentiment recovery in this scenario is slow and uneven.

**Alternative (25%):** Prolonged conflict → entrenched stagflation → structural sentiment shift. Markets reprice for a world of permanently higher energy costs, elevated defense spending, and slower growth. This is the secular bear scenario that would redefine asset allocation for years.

**Sentiment trajectory:** Pessimism → Caution → Cautious Optimism (resolution) or Pessimism → Fear → Structural Pessimism (prolonged conflict).

## Sentiment-Driven Risk Assessment

### Tail Risks (Low Probability, High Impact)

1. **Hormuz Closure (20-25%):** Oil spikes to $150+, global supply chain disruption, equity crash 15-20%, VIX >40, HY spreads >500bps. Sentiment: sheer panic. This is the scenario where fear becomes self-fulfilling — margin calls, forced selling, liquidity vacuum.

2. **Credit Cascade (15-20%):** HY defaults rising from energy sector exposure → IG contagion → liquidity squeeze → systemic risk. Sentiment: fear morphs into frozen markets. The Fed would be forced to intervene despite inflation concerns.

3. **Fed Policy Error (20-25%):** Premature rate cut in response to growth scare → inflation re-accelerates → credibility loss → stagflation expectations become entrenched. Sentiment: confusion giving way to despair as policy loses its anchor.

4. **Dollar Crisis (10-15%):** DXY weakness (–3% YoY despite hawkish Fed) is a warning. If foreign holders reduce Treasury exposure due to fiscal deficit concerns + geopolitical risk, the dollar could weaken sharply, importing more inflation and amplifying the stagflation dynamic.

### Behavioral Risk Factors

- **Panic selling threshold:** VIX >30 is the level where systematic strategies begin forced selling. We're at 24.5 — close but not there. A 20% move higher in VIX is achievable with a single negative Hormuz headline.
- **Margin call cascade:** Gold and silver's pullback (–10% and –16% MoM) may reflect forced liquidation. If equities decline further, leveraged positions across commodities and crypto face margin pressure.
- **Retail capitulation:** AAII at 4.8th percentile suggests retail has capitulated. Historically, this is a medium-term bullish signal — but only if the catalyst (war) resolves. In a worsening conflict, retail selling has further to go.

## Contrarian Opportunities

1. **Long Gold on Weakness:** Gold pulled back 10% from highs despite the conflict intensifying. This is margin-call selling, not thesis change. If the Hormuz situation deteriorates, gold is the primary beneficiary. **Contrarian signal: Strong.** The crowd's fear is real, but gold's temporary weakness is a positioning anomaly, not a sentiment shift.

2. **Short Oil (Tactical):** WTI RSI at 69, +68% in one month. The pace of gains is unsustainable. A tactical short with tight stops could profit from a mean-reversion snap — particularly if OPEC+ output hikes materialize or Hormuz rhetoric softens. **Contrarian signal: Moderate.** The trend is your friend until it isn't, and $111 oil has momentum.

3. **Long Equities Post-Washout:** If the Hormuz deadline triggers a sharp equity sell-off (S&P <6,200), the combination of extreme fear (13/100), oversold conditions, and a potential diplomatic backstop creates a high-risk-reward entry. **Contrarian signal: Conditional.** Only after a capitulation event, not before.

4. **Avoid: Long Crypto as Inflation Hedge:** Bitcoin is tracking risk assets, not gold. The crowd that positioned BTC as "digital gold" is being proven wrong. No contrarian signal here — the thesis is genuinely broken in this regime.

### Where the Crowd Is Right and You Shouldn't Fade

- **Fear of stagflation:** The crowd's fear of sustained inflation + slowing growth is data-driven and justified. Don't fade this.
- **Credit caution:** HY spreads widening is a rational response to energy costs impacting corporate borrowers. Respect this signal.
- **Defensive positioning:** Institutions reducing equity exposure is prudent given the uncertainty. This is not overcrowded — there's still significant equity positioning to unwind.
